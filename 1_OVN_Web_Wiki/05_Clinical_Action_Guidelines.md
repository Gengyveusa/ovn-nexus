# Clinical Action Guidelines — "Do Now" vs "Do Not Claim"

> **Source:** ovnnexus.com homepage + /science
> **Audience:** Dental clinicians, periodontists, cardiometabolic physicians, geriatricians

---

## Framing Statement

> "The evidence does not yet support claiming periodontal disease as a validated causal driver of ASCVD, cancer, or Alzheimer's disease. But there is plenty of justified clinical action right now."

This framework separates **what clinicians can responsibly do today** from **what they must not claim** — grounded in the [[02_Evidence_Tiers]] system.

---

## DO — Supported Clinical Actions

### 1. Treat Periodontitis as a Systemic Health Issue

**Evidence basis:** Tier 1 (Established)

- Periodontitis is not "just a local dental problem"
- Systemic inflammatory burden is measurable and documented
- Treat with the understanding that oral health has body-wide implications
- Frame for patients: "Your gum health affects your overall health"

### 2. Capture Periodontal Status in Medical Histories

**Evidence basis:** Tier 1 (Established)

- Include periodontal assessment in **cardiometabolic** evaluations
- Include in **neurological/geriatric** assessments
- Document: probing depths, BOP, radiographic bone loss, periodontal diagnosis
- Share findings with medical co-management team

### 3. Anticipate Biomarker Improvement After Therapy

**Evidence basis:** Tier 1 (Established)

- Communicate expected improvements in surrogate markers:
  - **hsCRP** (high-sensitivity C-reactive protein) — systemic inflammation
  - **IL-6** (interleukin-6) — pro-inflammatory cytokine
  - **Endothelial function** markers
- Use as motivation for treatment adherence
- Consider pre/post-treatment biomarker tracking

---

## DO NOT — Claims to Avoid

### Primary Prohibition

> **"Do NOT claim periodontal disease is a proven causal driver of ASCVD, cancer, or Alzheimer's disease — the causal evidence is not yet there."**

### Specific Claims to Avoid

| Claim | Why It's Wrong | Actual Status |
|-------|---------------|---------------|
| "Gum disease **causes** heart attacks" | Causation not established; association documented | Tier 1 association, Tier 3 causation |
| "Treating gum disease **prevents** Alzheimer's" | No prospective interventional evidence | Tier 3 hypothesis |
| "Gingipain inhibitors **slow** dementia" | One failed clinical trial; no validated therapy | Tier 3 hypothesis |
| "The AHA says periodontal disease causes CVD" | AHA 2012 statement explicitly states association ≠ causation | Misrepresentation of Tier 1 evidence |
| "Cleaning your teeth **cures** systemic inflammation" | Overstates intervention effects | Tier 1 shows improvement in markers, not cure |

## Language Guide

### Appropriate Language (Tier-Matched)

| Tier | Acceptable Phrasing |
|------|---------------------|
| **Tier 1** | "Periodontitis is associated with..." / "Evidence shows that periodontal therapy improves..." |
| **Tier 2** | "Preclinical evidence suggests..." / "Laboratory studies indicate that OMVs can..." |
| **Tier 3** | "A working hypothesis proposes..." / "Under active investigation..." / "It is hypothesized that..." |

### Red-Flag Language (Avoid)

- "Causes" / "proven to cause" (unless referring to local tissue destruction)
- "Cures" / "prevents" (overstates intervention evidence)
- "Breakthrough" / "revolutionary" (sensationalizes)
- "The science is settled" (it isn't, beyond Tier 1 associations)

## Clinical Workflow Integration

### For Periodontists
1. Document systemic risk factors alongside periodontal diagnosis
2. Order baseline hsCRP/IL-6 when co-managing with physicians
3. Re-measure biomarkers 3–6 months post-definitive therapy
4. Share results with medical team for integrated care

### For General Dentists
1. Screen for periodontal disease at every hygiene visit
2. Ask about cardiovascular, metabolic, and cognitive health history
3. Refer complex cases to periodontist with systemic context
4. Educate patients using Tier 1 language only

### For Physicians (Cardiology, Neurology, Geriatrics)
1. Ask about periodontal status in intake assessments
2. Consider dental referral for patients with elevated inflammatory markers of unclear origin
3. Recognize periodontal therapy as a potential adjunct to systemic management
4. Do not attribute systemic disease to oral infection without supporting evidence

## Patient Communication Templates

### What You CAN Say
> "Research shows that gum disease is linked to higher levels of inflammation throughout your body. Treating it can help reduce those inflammatory markers. That's one more reason to take your gum health seriously."

### What You Should NOT Say
> ~~"Your gum disease is causing your heart problems."~~
> ~~"If we fix your gums, we can prevent Alzheimer's."~~
