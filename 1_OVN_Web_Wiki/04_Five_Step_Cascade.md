# The Proposed 5-Step OMV-Driven Cascade

> **Source:** ovnnexus.com homepage + /science
> **Evidence tier:** Steps 1–2 are Tier 2; Steps 3–5 are Tier 3 — see [[02_Evidence_Tiers]]

---

## Overview

The 5-step cascade is the central mechanistic hypothesis of the OVN Axis framework. It proposes a conserved sequence by which oral bacterial OMVs initiate systemic pathology across multiple tissue types (vascular, neural, oncological).

```
Step 1          Step 2              Step 3              Step 4            Step 5
Barrier    →   Mitochondrial   →   Phenotypic      →   Secondary    →   Tissue
Disruption     Dysfunction         Reprogramming       EV Signals       Endpoints
```

---

## Step 1: Barrier Disruption

**Evidence level:** Preclinical + Translational (Tier 2)

### Mechanism
*P. gingivalis* cells and OMVs breach the periodontal epithelial barrier and enter systemic circulation.

### Key Processes
- **Junctional epithelium degradation:** Gingipains proteolytically cleave cell-cell adhesion molecules (E-cadherin, occludin)
- **Endothelial signaling:** OMVs increase endothelial activation markers (ICAM-1, VCAM-1, E-selectin)
- **Monocyte adhesion:** Activated endothelium recruits circulating monocytes
- **Epithelial integrity loss:** Tight junction disruption facilitates transcytosis

### Clinical Correlate
Ulcerated periodontal pocket epithelium (estimated surface area equivalent to palm of hand in severe periodontitis) provides direct vascular access for OMVs.

---

## Step 2: Mitochondrial Dysfunction

**Evidence level:** High-value mechanistic node (Tier 2)

### Mechanism
OMV virulence cargo impairs mitochondrial respiration in target cells, creating bioenergetic crisis.

### Key Processes
- **Mitochondrial fragmentation:** Drp1-dependent fission triggered by OMV cargo
- **ROS generation:** Increased reactive oxygen species from dysfunctional electron transport chain
- **Membrane potential collapse:** ΔΨm reduction in exposed endothelial cells
- **ATP depletion:** Reduced oxidative phosphorylation capacity
- **Metabolic switch:** Cells shift toward glycolytic metabolism (Warburg-like effect)

### Why This Step Is Critical
Mitochondrial dysfunction is the **convergence node** — it connects upstream bacterial insult to downstream phenotypic changes. Multiple disease pathways (cardiovascular, neurodegenerative, oncological) share mitochondrial dysfunction as a common intermediate.

---

## Step 3: Phenotypic Reprogramming

**Evidence level:** Selected tissues (Tier 2–3)

### Mechanism
OMV-driven mitochondrial stress triggers host cells to adopt altered phenotypes that promote disease.

### Tissue-Specific Reprogramming

| Target Tissue | Phenotypic Shift | Consequence |
|--------------|------------------|-------------|
| **Vascular smooth muscle** | Contractile → osteogenic/calcifying | Vascular calcification, arterial stiffening |
| **Oral epithelium** | Epithelial → mesenchymal (EMT-like) | Tissue remodeling, potential cancer initiation |
| **Microglia** | Surveillant → inflammatory (M1-like) | Neuroinflammation, synaptic pruning, neurodegeneration |
| **Endothelial cells** | Quiescent → activated/dysfunctional | Atherosclerotic plaque initiation |
| **Macrophages** | Resolving → pro-inflammatory | Sustained inflammatory signaling |

### Key Concept: Phenotypic Plasticity
These are not mutations but **epigenetic/metabolic reprogramming events** — cells change their behavior in response to environmental signals (OMV cargo + mitochondrial stress). This plasticity may be partially reversible with intervention.

---

## Step 4: Secondary EV Signals

**Evidence level:** Emerging (Tier 3)

### Mechanism
Reprogrammed host cells shed their own extracellular vesicles (EVs) with altered cargo, amplifying and propagating the pathological signal.

### Key Processes
- **Altered host EV release:** *P. gingivalis* infection changes the quantity and cargo of EVs released by host cells
- **Signal amplification:** One OMV-affected cell produces many secondary EVs → exponential signal expansion
- **Remote propagation:** Host EVs travel via blood to distant tissues not directly exposed to bacterial OMVs
- **Mitochondrial transfer:** EVs can carry mitochondrial components; documented in cancer contexts affecting chemoresistance
- **Alzheimer's models:** Host EVs propagate pathology in AD model systems

### Critical Distinction
| | Bacterial OMVs (Step 1) | Host EVs (Step 4) |
|--|------------------------|-------------------|
| **Origin** | *P. gingivalis* outer membrane | Host cell plasma membrane |
| **Cargo** | Gingipains, LPS, PPAD | Altered host proteins, miRNA, mtDNA |
| **Trigger** | Constitutive bacterial shedding | Response to OMV-induced stress |
| **Reach** | Oral → systemic | Systemic → distant tissues |
| **Amplification** | Limited by bacterial load | Exponential (cell-to-cell relay) |

---

## Step 5: Tissue-Level Endpoints

**Evidence level:** Guardrail (Tier 2–3)

### Mechanism
Cumulative effects of the cascade manifest as tissue-specific pathology.

### Documented Endpoints (Animal Models)

| Tissue System | Endpoint | Model Evidence |
|--------------|----------|----------------|
| **Vascular** | Atherosclerotic plaque, vascular calcification | Mouse/rabbit models of *P. gingivalis*-accelerated atherosclerosis |
| **Oncological** | Cancer progression in oral/GI tissues | *F. nucleatum* and *P. gingivalis* in colorectal/oral cancer models |
| **Neurological** | Alzheimer's-like pathology (amyloid, tau, neuroinflammation) | Gingipain detection in human AD brain tissue; mouse AD models |

### Critical Caveat
> Endpoints "remain tissue-specific rather than identical across systems." The cascade is proposed as **conserved in mechanism** (Steps 1–4) but **divergent in manifestation** (Step 5). This is a hypothesis — cross-tissue conservation is not proven.
