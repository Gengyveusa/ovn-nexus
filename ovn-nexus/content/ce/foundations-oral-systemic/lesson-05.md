## Why this lesson exists

Of the three arms of the OVN Axis, the vascular arm is the one that carries the most human data — and, for exactly that reason, the one most easily overstated. Cardiovascular disease is the leading cause of death worldwide, roughly **32% of global deaths**. Periodontitis is one of the most common chronic infections on the planet. Put those two facts next to each other and the temptation is enormous: to draw a straight causal line from the inflamed pocket to the blocked coronary and tell the patient that cleaning the first will protect the second.

This lesson walks that line without crossing it. The vascular arm is where the mechanism is best characterized — endothelial activation, the nitric-oxide axis, shared inflammatory readouts — and where the intervention data are most encouraging. It is also where the single most important sentence in this entire course applies most sharply: periodontal therapy demonstrably improves inflammatory and endothelial markers, and it has *not* been shown to prevent heart attacks. Holding both halves of that sentence at once is the whole skill.

> "The opportunity is not to collapse diseases into one disease, but to identify shared upstream interfaces worth measuring and testing."

## From the pocket to the endothelium

Recall the geometry from Lesson 1. The ulcerated periodontal pocket is a bleeding, colonized wound sitting directly against gingival capillaries, and three things cross that boundary: whole organisms, bacterial products such as lipopolysaccharide (LPS), and outer membrane vesicles (OMVs) shed by gram-negative pathogens like *Porphyromonas gingivalis*. The vascular arm begins the moment those cargoes reach the bloodstream and meet the endothelium — the single-cell lining of every vessel in the body.

A healthy endothelium is quiescent, anti-adhesive, and vasodilatory. Chronic exposure to circulating inflammatory signals changes that. The endothelium becomes **activated**: it switches on a program of surface adhesion molecules that turns a smooth, non-stick surface into flypaper for circulating leukocytes.

| Adhesion molecule | Full name | Role in the vascular arm |
|-------------------|-----------|--------------------------|
| **ICAM-1** | Intercellular adhesion molecule-1 | Firm adhesion of monocytes to the endothelial surface |
| **VCAM-1** | Vascular cell adhesion molecule-1 | Selective capture of monocytes and lymphocytes |
| **E-selectin** | Endothelial selectin | Initial rolling and tethering of leukocytes |

The sequence that follows is the textbook opening of atherogenesis: leukocytes roll (E-selectin), then adhere firmly (ICAM-1, VCAM-1), then transmigrate into the vessel wall. Once inside the intima, monocytes differentiate into macrophages, take up oxidized lipid, and become the foam cells that seed the atherosclerotic plaque. Sustained low-grade inflammatory tone — the kind periodontitis is documented to produce — keeps this activation program idling, and over years contributes to **vascular remodeling**: a stiffer, thicker, more inflamed arterial wall.

> Endothelial activation is not a metaphor. It is a measurable phenotype — upregulated ICAM-1, VCAM-1, and E-selectin — and it is the recognized first step in atherosclerosis.

## The nitric-oxide axis

If adhesion molecules describe how the endothelium turns *sticky*, the nitric-oxide axis describes how it loses its ability to relax. Nitric oxide (NO), produced by endothelial NO synthase (eNOS), is the master vasodilator: it keeps vessels supple, restrains platelet aggregation, and suppresses the very adhesion-molecule program described above. A healthy endothelium is, in large part, a well-supplied NO factory.

Inflammation degrades that factory. Pro-inflammatory cytokines and reactive oxygen species reduce NO bioavailability — partly by lowering eNOS activity, partly by chemically scavenging NO once it is made. The clinical consequence is **endothelial dysfunction**: vessels that dilate poorly when they should. This is not an abstraction. It is measurable at the bedside as **flow-mediated dilation (FMD)** — an ultrasound assay of how much a vessel widens after a brief occlusion — and it is one of the sturdiest bridges between the dental and vascular worlds, because FMD has been shown to *improve after periodontal therapy* in controlled settings.

So the vascular arm has two coupled failures. Endothelial *activation* (adhesion molecules up) and endothelial *dysfunction* (NO down) are two faces of the same inflamed endothelium, and periodontal inflammation is a documented contributor to the inflammatory milieu that drives both.

```
   Periodontal inflammation
   (P. gingivalis, LPS, OMVs)
              │
              ▼
   Circulating inflammatory signals
   (IL-6 → hs-CRP; direct OMV effects)
              │
        ┌─────┴─────┐
        ▼           ▼
   Endothelial   NO axis
   activation    suppressed
   (ICAM-1,      (↓ eNOS,
   VCAM-1,       ↓ NO
   E-selectin)   bioavailability)
        │           │
        └─────┬─────┘
              ▼
   Monocyte adhesion, foam cells,
   vascular remodeling
              │
              ▼
   Atherosclerotic plaque  →  ASCVD endpoints
```

Read the diagram with the same discipline the course demands elsewhere. The upper and middle rows — the inflammatory readouts and the endothelial phenotypes — rest on established human and mechanistic data. The bottom arrow, from remodeling to a clinical ASCVD *event*, is an association across populations, not a demonstrated causal chain you can promise to interrupt.

## Shared readouts: hs-CRP and IL-6

The reason the vascular arm is clinically tractable at all is that oral inflammation and cardiovascular risk are read on the *same instruments*. You do not need an OMV assay to act; you need the two serum markers you already know.

- **hs-CRP (high-sensitivity C-reactive protein).** The gold-standard serum marker of systemic inflammation, produced by the liver under the drive of IL-6. Interpret it with the familiar cardiovascular-risk bands: **< 1 mg/L low, 1–3 mg/L average, > 3 mg/L high**. An hs-CRP **> 3.0 mg/L** marks elevated vascular risk — and it is also the threshold above which periodontitis-associated inflammation reliably shows up.
- **IL-6 (interleukin-6).** The upstream pro-inflammatory cytokine that drives hepatic CRP production. Useful as a corroborating marker; less standardized than hs-CRP for routine banding.

That these are the *same* markers used to stratify cardiovascular risk is the entire clinical opening. Periodontitis is a modifiable, identifiable contributor to the low-grade inflammatory tone that hs-CRP measures — and, critically, **periodontal therapy improves the marker**. Randomized trials of periodontal treatment show hs-CRP commonly dropping on the order of **~20–30% at 3–6 months** post-definitive therapy, alongside measurable improvement in endothelial markers and FMD.

| Marker / readout | What it reads | Effect of periodontal therapy | Tier |
|------------------|---------------|-------------------------------|------|
| **hs-CRP** | Systemic inflammatory tone | ↓ ~20–30% at 3–6 mo | Established |
| **IL-6** | Upstream inflammatory drive | ↓ (corroborating) | Established |
| **FMD** | Endothelial (NO-dependent) function | Improves post-therapy | Supported |
| **ICAM-1 / VCAM-1 / E-selectin** | Endothelial activation | Improve in studies | Supported |

The caveat from Lesson 2 carries over unchanged: hs-CRP is non-specific. It rises with any inflammatory stimulus — obesity, infection, recent illness, autoimmune flare — so a single elevated value never implicates the mouth by itself. The periodontal exam earns its place precisely because it identifies *one candidate contributor you can actually treat*, and the re-measurement tells you whether treating it moved the number.

## Periodontitis and ASCVD: what the data do and do not show

Now the load-bearing claim, stated with care. Across large cohorts and multiple meta-analyses, **periodontitis is associated with atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease (ASCVD)**. That association survives adjustment for many shared risk factors, and it is buttressed by a coherent, well-characterized mechanism — the endothelial activation and NO-axis story above. Association plus mechanism is a strong combination. It is enough to justify treating periodontitis as a systemic-health matter and folding periodontal status into cardiometabolic assessment.

What that evidence does **not** establish is that treating periodontitis *prevents cardiovascular events*. The intervention data are consistent and encouraging on **surrogate markers** — hs-CRP falls, IL-6 falls, endothelial function improves — but no trial has been powered and designed to demonstrate that periodontal therapy reduces hard endpoints like myocardial infarction, stroke, or cardiovascular death. Periodontitis and ASCVD also share a long list of confounders — smoking, diabetes, age, socioeconomic status — and disentangling a genuine causal contribution from shared background risk is exactly the work that remains undone.

> Evidence shows periodontal therapy *improves inflammatory and endothelial markers.* It does **not** show periodontal therapy *prevents heart attacks.* Say the first; never say the second.

This is the cardinal rule of the vascular arm, and it maps cleanly onto the evidence tiers.

| Claim | Tier | Verdict |
|-------|------|---------|
| "Periodontitis is associated with elevated hs-CRP and IL-6." | 1 — Established | Say it. |
| "Periodontitis is associated with ASCVD across large cohorts." | 1 — Established (association) | Say it — as association, not causation. |
| "Endothelial activation and NO-axis dysfunction are the plausible mechanism." | 1–2 — Established / Supported | Discuss as mechanism, not proven event-prevention. |
| "Periodontal therapy lowers hs-CRP ~20–30% and improves FMD." | 1 — Established | Say it. |
| "*P. gingivalis* OMVs directly activate endothelium / stress mitochondria." | 2 — Supported | "Preclinical evidence suggests…" |
| "Treating gum disease prevents heart attacks or strokes." | 3 — Hypothesis | Do NOT say it. |

## The cardinal rule at the chairside

Translate this into what you actually do. You can identify periodontitis on exam. You can, in co-management with the patient's physician, document a shared vascular-inflammatory readout — hs-CRP, ± IL-6 — particularly in a patient with Stage III–IV disease or an otherwise unexplained hs-CRP > 3.0 mg/L who reports bleeding gums. You can treat the periodontitis and re-measure the marker at 3–6 months, expecting the ~20–30% drop. What you cannot do — and must not imply — is tell that patient their heart attack has been prevented.

The measurement-first sequence the framework insists on applies here in its purest form:

> Prove an upstream signal, show that intervention moves it, and only then claim disease modification.

For the vascular arm, the first two steps are already walkable in the clinic today. The third — disease modification, event prevention — is a hypothesis awaiting prospective human trials, and the professionalism of this field depends on not speaking as though it has already arrived.

## The bottom line

The oral–vascular link is the strongest arm of the OVN Axis and, precisely because of that strength, the one most in need of discipline. The mechanism is coherent and largely established: periodontal inflammation feeds circulating IL-6 and hs-CRP, activates the endothelium through ICAM-1, VCAM-1, and E-selectin, suppresses the nitric-oxide axis, and contributes to the vascular remodeling that underlies atherosclerosis. The association with ASCVD is real and replicated. The surrogate-marker response to therapy is real and quantifiable — hs-CRP down ~20–30%, endothelial function improved.

And the ceiling is equally real. None of that is the same as preventing a cardiovascular event, and no honest clinician should say it is. The defensible claim is the modest one:

> Oral bacterial EVs are plausible systemic effectors and a tractable upstream biomarker interface — strong enough to justify measurement, intervention studies, and disciplined translational development now.

Measure the shared readout, treat the modifiable source, re-measure to prove the signal moved — and let the number, not a causal story you cannot yet defend, do the talking.
