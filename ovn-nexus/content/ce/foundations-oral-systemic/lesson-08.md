## Why this lesson exists

Every lesson before this one built a case. The mouth is not a silo; the periodontal exam is a graded index of systemic inflammatory exposure; a small number of serum markers make that exposure measurable; and a strict evidence-tier discipline keeps the whole framework honest. This lesson does something different. It converts the case into behavior — the specific things a dentist, hygienist, or physician can do at the chair on Monday morning, and the specific words they can say while doing them.

That second half matters as much as the first. A clinician who acts correctly but speaks carelessly can undo the credibility of the entire field in a single sentence to a worried patient. The skill this course has been building toward is not merely *knowing* the science; it is **acting on exactly what the evidence supports and claiming nothing more.** The gap between those two — between defensible action and indefensible claim — is where careers and reputations are made or lost.

> The evidence does not yet support claiming periodontal disease as a validated causal driver of ASCVD, cancer, or Alzheimer's disease. But there is plenty of justified clinical action right now.

Hold both halves of that sentence at once. This lesson is the synthesis: the measurement-first strategy applied at the chair, the defensible-action list, the tier-matched scripts, a standalone co-management letter you can adapt today, and an audit checklist to run against your own words before they leave your mouth.

## The measurement-first strategy, applied at the chair

The framework's answer to "what do we do while causation is still unproven?" is a sequence, not a slogan: **prove an upstream signal, show that intervention moves it, and only then discuss disease modification.** That sequence is not abstract. It is a three-step loop you can walk with a real patient this week.

1. **Identify.** Diagnose and stage periodontitis using the tools you already have — probing depth, bleeding on probing, radiographic bone loss. Stage III–IV disease is your trigger for systemic co-management; Stage I–II is documented and monitored.
2. **Treat.** Reduce bacterial load and inflammatory signaling at the source. Scaling and root planing is the foundational, thoroughly Established move; surgical intervention where indicated. You are quieting the transmitter, not chasing a distant organ.
3. **Re-measure.** In co-management, document a baseline systemic inflammatory signal (hs-CRP ± IL-6), ensure the periodontitis is actually treated, and re-check the marker at **3–6 months.** Randomized trials show hs-CRP commonly falls **~20–30%** after definitive therapy — an honest, evidence-based expectation to set.

> Prove an upstream signal, show that intervention moves it, and only then claim disease modification. That path is scientifically stronger and commercially more credible.

Notice what this loop lets you say and what it forbids. You can tell a patient you *measured* their inflammation, *treated* a modifiable source, and *watched the number move.* You cannot tell them you prevented a heart attack. The loop produces defensible evidence of an effect on a surrogate marker — and stops precisely there.

## The defensible-action list

Here is the concrete separation between what you can do today and what you must not promise. The left column is Tier 1, safe to act on. The right column is the set of claims that outrun the evidence — the ones that convert credible medicine into hype.

| Do this now (Established) | Do not promise this |
|---------------------------|---------------------|
| Treat periodontitis as a systemic health issue, not "just a local dental problem" | Do not say treating gums *prevents* heart attacks |
| Capture periodontal status (PD, BOP, bone loss, stage-grade) in cardiometabolic, neurological, and geriatric histories | Do not say treating gums *prevents or slows* dementia |
| Order baseline hs-CRP (± IL-6) when co-managing Stage III–IV disease | Do not say periodontal therapy *cures* systemic inflammation |
| Re-measure hs-CRP at 3–6 months post-definitive therapy and document the change | Do not claim gingipain inhibitors are a *validated* dementia therapy |
| Frame for the patient: "Your gum health affects your overall health" | Do not tell a patient their gum disease *is causing* their systemic disease |

The discipline is asymmetric on purpose. Every item on the left rests on meta-analyses and replicated human data. Every item on the right requires prospective interventional trials that do not yet exist. When in doubt, act from the left column and speak in its language.

## Tier-matched patient communication

Patients do not hear tiers; they hear promises and fears. Your job is to translate accurate evidence into words that inform without overclaiming. The rule is simple: **match the strength of your language to the strength of the evidence.**

For anything you can act on today, use associational, effect-on-marker language:

> "Research shows gum disease is linked to higher levels of inflammation throughout your body. Treating it can help lower those inflammatory markers. That's one more reason to take your gum health seriously."

That sentence is entirely Tier 1. It states an association ("linked to"), describes a real and measurable effect ("lower those inflammatory markers"), and motivates action — without asserting a prevented disease. Contrast it with the sentences you must never say:

> ~~"Your gum disease is causing your heart problems."~~
> ~~"If we fix your gums, we can prevent Alzheimer's."~~

When a patient or family arrives already carrying a Tier-3 causal story — "I read online that gum disease causes Alzheimer's, will treating it save my mother?" — your task is honest de-escalation, not agreement. You cannot promise a cognitive benefit and should not imply one. What you *can* do is recommend periodontal treatment on its own solid merits: comfort, function, nutrition, and reduction of a documented inflammatory exposure. That is reason enough, and it is true.

The vocabulary discipline generalizes across all three tiers:

| Tier | Acceptable phrasing |
|------|---------------------|
| **Established** | "Periodontitis is associated with…" / "Periodontal therapy improves…" |
| **Supported** | "Preclinical evidence suggests…" / "Laboratory studies indicate…" |
| **Hypothesis** | "A working hypothesis proposes…" / "Under active investigation…" |

And the red-flag words that should trigger a self-correction before you finish the sentence: *causes*, *cures*, *prevents*, *breakthrough*, *the science is settled.* Each of them promises more than Tier 1 can deliver.

## The co-management letter, as a teachable artifact

The single highest-yield workflow change in this entire course is closing the communication loop between the dental chair and the medical chart. The instrument for that is a co-management letter — and it deserves to be taught as a standalone artifact, because its structure *is* the tier discipline made concrete.

A defensible letter does four things, in order: it **reports** objective findings; it **states** the association in plain, sourced language; it **disclaims** causation explicitly; and it **requests** concrete, defensible actions. Drop any one of those four and the letter fails — omit the disclaimer and you have overclaimed; omit the requests and you have merely gossiped about a patient. All four, every time.

```
[Dental Practice Letterhead]
Date: ____________

To: Dr. ____________ (Primary Care)
Re: [Patient name], DOB ____________

Dear Dr. ____________,

Your patient is under my care for periodontal disease. I am writing to
share findings relevant to their systemic inflammatory status, for your
awareness and possible co-management.

PERIODONTAL FINDINGS (objective)
  - Diagnosis / Stage-Grade: ____________ (e.g., Stage III, Grade B)
  - Probing depths: ____ sites >= 5 mm; deepest ____ mm
  - Bleeding on probing: ____ %
  - Radiographic bone loss: ____________

CLINICAL CONTEXT (evidence-based, associational)
  Periodontitis is associated with elevated systemic inflammatory
  markers (hs-CRP, IL-6). Periodontal therapy has been shown to improve
  these surrogate markers, with hs-CRP commonly falling ~20-30% at
  3-6 months post-therapy.

CAUSATION DISCLAIMER (explicit)
  I am NOT asserting that this patient's periodontal status causes any
  systemic disease. I am flagging a modifiable inflammatory exposure
  for your consideration.

REQUESTS / SUGGESTIONS (defensible, concrete)
  [ ] Consider baseline hs-CRP (+/- IL-6) if not recently obtained.
  [ ] We are initiating/continuing active periodontal therapy.
  [ ] Suggest re-checking hs-CRP at 3-6 months to track response.
  [ ] Please share relevant cardiometabolic history for coordinated care.

I am happy to coordinate. Thank you for co-managing this patient.

Sincerely,
Dr. ____________, DDS
Contact: ____________
```

Read the letter as a physician would. It never asks the PCP to believe anything unproven. It hands over data the physician can verify, frames it in language the physician already trusts, and requests actions — a serum marker, a shared history, a follow-up measurement — that are individually defensible regardless of what the oral-systemic causal question ultimately resolves to. That is why it works: it earns co-management by demanding nothing on faith.

> A letter that reports, states the association, disclaims causation, and requests defensible actions is credible by construction. A letter that skips the disclaimer is a liability by construction.

The same four-part skeleton scales in either direction. A hygienist referring a complex case to a periodontist uses it with the systemic context attached. A periodontist writing to a cardiologist uses it verbatim. A physician documenting a dental referral in the chart can mirror it back. The artifact is portable; the discipline travels with it.

## An evidence-tier audit checklist

Before any statement reaches a patient, a colleague, a chart, or a letter, run it through this audit. This is not a formality — sorting your own claims by tier is a formal objective of this course, and it is the single habit that separates credible clinical education from marketing.

| Audit question | Pass condition |
|----------------|----------------|
| Have I named the tier of this claim before saying it? | Every claim maps to Established, Supported, or Hypothesis |
| Am I using tier-matched verbs? | "associated / improves" for Tier 1; "suggests / indicates" for Tier 2; "proposes / under investigation" for Tier 3 |
| Have I avoided the red-flag words? | No *causes*, *cures*, *prevents*, *breakthrough*, "settled" attached to an unproven claim |
| If I stated an association, did I stop there? | No slide from "associated with ASCVD" into "causes ASCVD" |
| For any patient-facing benefit, is it a marker effect, not a prevented event? | "Lowers inflammatory markers," not "prevents your heart attack" |
| In a co-management letter, are all four parts present? | Report + association + causation disclaimer + defensible request |
| Could the recipient act on this without believing anything unproven? | Every requested action is independently Tier 1 |

Work the checklist top to bottom and one failure mode dominates: the quiet slide from association to causation. It is the most common error in this field, and it usually happens mid-sentence, unnoticed, in the reassuring space between what a patient wants to hear and what you can honestly say. The audit exists to catch it there.

## Tying the course together

Step back and the arc is clean. Lesson 1 established that periodontitis generates a measurable systemic inflammatory signal — Tier 1, the solid brick. The middle lessons built the mechanism, the routes out of the mouth, and the markers that make the signal visible. This lesson closes the loop by turning all of it into two things a clinician actually controls: **an action** and **a sentence.**

The action is the measurement-first loop — identify, treat, re-measure — and it is defensible today because every step rests on established human data. The sentence is whatever you say about that loop, and it is defensible only if it never claims more than the loop demonstrates. A dropped hs-CRP is real. A prevented heart attack is a hypothesis. Both facts are true at once, and the professionalism of this field lives entirely in keeping them straight.

> You can hold two ideas at once: the association between periodontitis and systemic inflammation is Tier 1 and clinically actionable today; the claim that treating gums prevents systemic disease is Tier 3 and must be spoken of as a hypothesis. Confusing them is the one failure this course exists to prevent.

## The bottom line

Monday morning does not require believing more than the evidence supports. It requires a stage, a marker, a referral, a follow-up measurement, and a vocabulary matched to the strength of the data. Identify the periodontitis, treat the source, order the baseline, re-measure at 3–6 months, and write the letter that reports, states the association, disclaims causation, and requests defensible actions. Then let the number — not a causal story you cannot yet defend — do the talking. That is the whole discipline, and it is enough to put the mouth back into the conversation about whole-body health without ever overclaiming your way out of it.
