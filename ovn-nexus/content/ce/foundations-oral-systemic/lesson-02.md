## Why this lesson exists

Lesson 1 established that the mouth is not a silo — that a common periodontal infection can convert into a measurable systemic exposure. That leaves two questions a careful clinician will immediately ask. *By what route?* And *how sure are we?*

This lesson answers both, and it treats them as inseparable. It is easy to draw an arrow from the mouth to the heart, or the brain, or the immune system, and much harder to say honestly what that arrow is made of — a proven causal chain, a plausible mechanism, or a hopeful hypothesis. A framework that only draws the arrows is marketing. A framework that also grades them is science.

So we do two things at once here. First, we lay out the **Oral–Vascular–Neural (OVN) Axis**: three pathways out of the mouth, each terminating at a different systemic endpoint. Second, we introduce the **three-tier evidence discipline** that travels with every claim you will make about those pathways. By the end, you should be able to look at any oral-systemic statement — your own or someone else's — and answer both questions in one breath: *which route, and which tier.*

> A framework that draws the arrows is a diagram. A framework that grades them is a discipline. This course teaches the discipline.

## The OVN Axis: three routes out of the mouth

The OVN Axis is a proposed biological framework describing how chronic oral infection — particularly periodontitis driven by gram-negative pathogens like *Porphyromonas gingivalis* — produces systemic effects through three interconnected pathways. The candidate that ties them together is the **outer membrane vesicle (OMV)**: a nanoscale, cargo-laden particle shed by *P. gingivalis* that can cross barriers intact bacteria cannot. But the three routes are worth naming and grading separately, because they do not all carry the same weight of evidence.

Here is the map the whole course hangs on:

```
                    Oral Microbes & OMVs
                           |
                    +------+------+
                    v      v      v
              Bloodstream  CNS   Immune
              (Vascular)  (Neural) (Systemic)
                    |      |      |
                    v      v      v
                  Heart   Brain  Inflammation
                 (ASCVD)  (Neuro-  (Chronic
                          degen)   disease)
```

Read the diagram top to bottom and you have the mechanism. Read it left to right and you have three arms — each with its own crossing cargo, its own endpoint, and, crucially, its own confidence level.

**1. Oral → Vascular.** Bacterial products, and especially OMVs, enter the systemic circulation and act on the vascular endothelium — driving endothelial activation, inflammatory signaling, and vascular remodeling. The endpoint is cardiometabolic: atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease (ASCVD). This is the arm most relevant to the heart, and it rests on a large epidemiological association plus a growing preclinical mechanism.

**2. Oral → Neural.** Pathogen-derived cargo may reach the central nervous system by hematogenous spread or by direct neural routes — trigeminal or olfactory — contributing to neuroinflammation. The endpoint is neurodegenerative. *P. gingivalis* DNA and gingipains have been detected in Alzheimer's brain tissue; the observation is real, but its causal meaning is not settled. This is the arm to speak about most cautiously.

**3. Oral → Immune/Systemic.** Chronic periodontal inflammation elevates circulating hs-CRP and IL-6, producing a low-grade systemic inflammatory state. The endpoint is the broad family of chronic inflammatory disease. This is the **best-established** of the three routes — the one you can stand behind clinically today.

| Pathway | What crosses | Endpoint | Confidence of the arm |
|---------|-------------|----------|-----------------------|
| **Oral → Vascular** | OMVs, LPS, bacterial products acting on endothelium | ASCVD / cardiometabolic | Strong association + preclinical mechanism |
| **Oral → Neural** | Pathogen cargo via hematogenous or trigeminal/olfactory routes | Neurodegeneration | Preclinical + observational; weakest arm |
| **Oral → Immune/Systemic** | Sustained inflammatory signaling (hs-CRP, IL-6) | Chronic inflammatory disease | Best-established; clinically actionable |

Notice what the diagram deliberately does *not* say. It does not claim the mouth causes heart attacks, dementia, and chronic disease. It names three testable interfaces and holds each to the standard of evidence it has actually earned. That restraint is not a weakness of the framework — it is the framework.

## The evidence-tier discipline

Every claim you will encounter in this course is sorted into one of three tiers. Learning to sort claims yourself — and to speak in tier-matched language — is a formal objective of this lesson, because it is the single habit that separates credible clinical education from hype.

The principle underneath the tiers is plain: *not all science is created equal.* We present the current evidence with the same epistemic honesty you would apply to any clinical literature — and then we make that honesty audible by matching our spoken language to the tier.

**Tier 1 — ESTABLISHED.** High confidence, supported by meta-analyses and systematic reviews; replicated across studies and populations. Accepted by professional bodies, with caveats on causation. Safe to cite clinically. Read the caveat carefully: "established" refers to *association and mechanism*, not proven causation.

**Tier 2 — SUPPORTED, NOT YET SETTLED.** Moderate confidence; strong preclinical and mechanistic evidence, human data still emerging. Biologically plausible and actively studied, but not yet confirmed in human prospective trials. Discussable in educational contexts with appropriate caveats.

**Tier 3 — HYPOTHESIS UNDER ACTIVE TESTING.** Low-to-moderate confidence; a scientifically grounded but unproven framework with early evidence. Must be presented as a hypothesis, never as established fact, and requires prospective human data to validate.

The tiers are only useful if they change how you *talk*. Each tier comes with a language template, and the templates are not interchangeable:

| Tier | Confidence | Evidence base | Language to use |
|------|-----------|---------------|-----------------|
| **1 — Established** | High | Meta-analyses, systematic reviews | "…**is associated with**…" / "…**improves**…" |
| **2 — Supported** | Moderate | Preclinical, mechanistic models | "**Preclinical evidence suggests**…" / "Laboratory studies **indicate**…" |
| **3 — Hypothesis** | Low-to-moderate | Theoretical, early evidence | "**A working hypothesis proposes**…" / "**Under active investigation**…" |

> "Is associated with" is a Tier 1 phrase. "Preclinical evidence suggests" is a Tier 2 phrase. "A working hypothesis proposes" is a Tier 3 phrase. Using a Tier 1 verb for a Tier 3 idea is not enthusiasm — it is an error.

The tiers also gate action, not just speech. Only Tier 1 claims justify direct clinical action. Tier 2 claims justify awareness and monitoring. Tier 3 claims justify research participation — never a clinical promise.

## The cardinal rule

One sentence governs this entire field, and it is worth memorizing verbatim:

> Do NOT claim periodontal disease is a proven causal driver of ASCVD, cancer, or Alzheimer's disease — the causal evidence is not yet there.

You can hold two ideas at once without contradiction. The association between periodontitis and systemic inflammation is **Tier 1** and clinically actionable *today*. The claim that treating gums prevents heart attacks, tumors, or dementia is **Tier 3** and must be spoken of as a hypothesis. Collapsing the second into the first is the most common failure mode in oral-systemic communication — and avoiding it is precisely what makes the OVN framework worth teaching, and worth trusting.

Note how the axis diagram already encodes this. The vascular and systemic arms rest largely on established human association; the neural arm rests on preclinical and observational work. A well-disciplined clinician speaks about the three arms in three different registers — and never lets the excitement of the neural arm borrow the credibility of the systemic one.

## Worked examples: classifying claims

Classification becomes automatic with practice. Here are worked examples across all three tiers, drawn from claims you will meet later in the course. For each, note the tier *and* the language that fits it.

| Claim | Tier | Tier-matched phrasing |
|-------|------|----------------------|
| Periodontitis generates measurable systemic inflammatory exposure (↑ hs-CRP, IL-6) | **1** | "Periodontitis **is associated with** elevated systemic inflammation." |
| Periodontal therapy improves surrogate cardiometabolic markers | **1** | "Periodontal therapy **improves** endothelial and inflammatory markers." |
| *P. gingivalis* OMVs carry concentrated virulence cargo that injures host cells | **2** | "**Laboratory studies indicate** OMVs can injure host cells." |
| OMVs promote endothelial activation and mitochondrial stress | **2** | "**Preclinical evidence suggests** OMVs drive endothelial and mitochondrial stress." |
| A single conserved OMV-driven mechanism spans vascular, tumor, and brain disease | **3** | "**A working hypothesis proposes** a conserved cross-tissue mechanism." |
| Oral intervention alters long-term systemic disease trajectories | **3** | "Whether oral intervention changes disease trajectories is **under active investigation**." |

Two patterns are worth extracting. First, the tier tends to track the evidence *substrate*: human meta-analyses land at Tier 1, in-vitro and animal models at Tier 2, and cross-tissue or outcome-level extrapolations at Tier 3. Second, the verb does the work. "Improves" and "is associated with" are load-bearing Tier 1 verbs; the moment you reach for "suggests," "indicates," or "proposes," you have — correctly — dropped a tier.

## Try it yourself: a tier-classification exercise

Before moving on, classify each of the following five claims as Tier 1, 2, or 3, and rewrite it in tier-matched language. Work through them before reading the key.

1. "Patients with periodontitis show higher circulating IL-6 than periodontally healthy controls."
2. "OMVs from *P. gingivalis* translocate across an endothelial barrier in a laboratory model."
3. "Treating this patient's gum disease will lower their risk of a future heart attack."
4. "Salivary OMV signals could one day serve as a scalable upstream biomarker."
5. "Scaling and root planing improves endothelial function on follow-up testing."

<br>

> **Answer key.**
> **(1) Tier 1** — replicated human association. *"Periodontitis is associated with elevated circulating IL-6."*
> **(2) Tier 2** — preclinical barrier model. *"Preclinical evidence suggests OMVs can translocate across endothelial barriers."*
> **(3) Tier 3** — an outcome-level causal promise, and a violation of the cardinal rule if stated as fact. *"Whether periodontal treatment reduces cardiovascular events in an individual patient is under active investigation."*
> **(4) Tier 3** — early-stage assay development. *"A working hypothesis proposes that salivary OMV signals could function as upstream biomarkers."*
> **(5) Tier 1** — supported by interventional human data on surrogate markers. *"Periodontal therapy improves endothelial function."*

If you caught that claim 3 is the trap — a Tier 3 idea dressed in a Tier 1 promise — you have internalized the most important reflex in this course.

## The bottom line

The OVN Axis gives you a map: three routes out of the mouth — vascular to the heart, neural to the brain, immune to systemic inflammation — with the systemic arm the strongest and the neural arm the most tentative. The evidence-tier discipline gives you a language: Established, Supported, Hypothesis, each with a phrasing you are obligated to use.

Held together, they produce a clinician who can be both enthusiastic and honest — who can say what the evidence supports without saying one word more.

> The most credible thing you can say in this field is the true thing said at the right tier. "Periodontitis is associated with systemic inflammation" is worth more than any overstatement, precisely because it will still be true tomorrow.

Modest is not weak. A claim graded to its tier is a claim that survives scrutiny — and a clinician who speaks that way earns the standing to bring the mouth back into the conversation about whole-body health. The next lesson takes the strongest of these three arms and follows it into the biology of the OMV itself.
