## The exam you were taught to skip

The history and physical is medicine's oldest instrument, and almost every part of it survived into modern practice — except the mouth. Most adult H&Ps note "oropharynx clear" and move on. Yet the periodontal exam is the only routine examination that directly quantifies a chronic, modifiable, gram-negative infection sitting against the systemic circulation.

The argument is deliberately conservative. We are **not** asking you to attribute a patient's coronary disease or cognitive decline to their gums. We are asking you to treat the periodontal exam as what the evidence supports it being: a **graded index of systemic inflammatory exposure** — cheap, already collected by a colleague down the hall, and correlated with markers you already order.

> The evidence does not yet support claiming periodontal disease as a validated causal driver of ASCVD, cancer, or Alzheimer's disease. But there is plenty of justified clinical action right now.

## The periodontal exam as exposure quantification

A periodontist's chart is, in effect, a dosimeter for oral inflammatory exposure. Three findings do most of the work:

- **Probing depth (PD).** The depth of the gingival sulcus in millimeters. Deeper pockets mean more ulcerated, colonized surface area in contact with the vasculature — a proxy for the *size of the exposure surface*.
- **Bleeding on probing (BOP).** Whether tissue bleeds when gently probed. BOP marks *active inflammation and ulceration* — the wound is open right now. High-percentage BOP means the boundary is actively permeable.
- **Radiographic bone loss.** Alveolar bone destruction on films. This is the *cumulative, historical* record — how long and how severely the exposure has persisted.

Read together, these convert a dental chart into an estimate of inflammatory dose: how large the source is (PD), how active it is (BOP), and how chronic it has been (bone loss) — exactly the kind of graded exposure variable physicians are trained to reason about.

## Stage → systemic-risk → action

Contemporary periodontal classification stages disease I–IV, primarily by severity and complexity of bone loss. The table below maps each stage to its systemic-exposure reading and to a **defensible co-management action**. Note the evidence tiers embedded in the language: the *actions* are Tier 1 (order markers, treat, re-measure, refer); the *risk framing* stays associational.

| Stage | Periodontal picture | Systemic-exposure reading | Co-management action |
|-------|--------------------|--------------------------|----------------------|
| **I — Initial** | ~1–2 mm bone loss; localized BOP; PD ≤ 4 mm | Low, early exposure | Note in H&P; reinforce oral hygiene; no biomarker order needed on this basis alone |
| **II — Moderate** | Bone loss to ~15–33% of root; PD ≤ 5 mm | Modest ongoing exposure | Document periodontal status; if other cardiometabolic risk present, consider baseline hs-CRP |
| **III — Severe** | Bone loss to mid-root and beyond; PD ≥ 6 mm; possible tooth loss | Substantial, active exposure | Order **hs-CRP ± IL-6**; ensure active periodontal treatment is underway; co-manage with dental team |
| **IV — Advanced** | Extensive loss; masticatory dysfunction; multiple teeth lost | High, chronic exposure | Treat as a meaningful modifiable inflammatory contributor; hs-CRP/IL-6 at baseline and post-therapy; explicit dental–medical co-management |

The staging is not a risk-prediction equation, and it should never be presented to the patient as one. It is a triage aid: it tells you which patients carry enough oral inflammatory exposure to be worth an inexpensive serum marker and a note to their dentist.

## Biomarkers: what to order and how to read it

Two serum markers do the heavy lifting in co-management. Both are Tier 1 in this context — their elevation in periodontitis and their improvement after therapy are established.

- **hs-CRP (high-sensitivity C-reactive protein).** The gold-standard serum marker of systemic inflammation. Interpret with the familiar cardiovascular-risk bands: **< 1 mg/L low, 1–3 mg/L average, > 3 mg/L high**. An unexplained hs-CRP **> 3 mg/L** in a patient with Stage III–IV periodontitis is a reasonable prompt to loop in the dental team, because you may be looking at a modifiable source.
- **IL-6 (interleukin-6).** The upstream cytokine that drives hepatic CRP production. Useful as a corroborating marker where available; less standardized for routine clinical banding than hs-CRP.

**What to expect after periodontal therapy.** This is where you can set an honest, evidence-based expectation. Randomized trials of periodontal treatment show measurable improvement in inflammatory and endothelial markers, with hs-CRP commonly dropping on the order of **~20–30% at 3–6 months** post-definitive therapy. So the co-management loop is concrete: order a baseline, ensure the periodontitis is actually treated, and **re-measure hs-CRP (± IL-6) at 3–6 months.**

A crucial caveat: hs-CRP is non-specific. It rises with any inflammatory stimulus — infection, obesity, recent illness, autoimmune flare — so a single elevated value does not implicate the mouth. The value of the periodontal exam is that it identifies *one candidate contributor you can actually do something about*, and the re-measurement tells you whether treating it moved the number.

> Evidence shows periodontal therapy *improves inflammatory markers.* It does **not** show periodontal therapy *prevents heart attacks.* Say the first; never say the second.

## A ready dentist → PCP consult-letter template

The single highest-yield workflow change is closing the communication loop between the dental chair and the medical chart. Here is a template a dental office can send — and that you, as a physician, can recognize and act on. It is written entirely in tier-matched language.

```
[Dental Practice Letterhead]
Date: ____________

To: Dr. ____________ (Primary Care)
Re: [Patient name], DOB ____________

Dear Dr. ____________,

Your patient is under my care for periodontal disease. I am writing to
share findings relevant to their systemic inflammatory status, for your
awareness and possible co-management.

PERIODONTAL FINDINGS
  - Diagnosis / Stage-Grade: ____________ (e.g., Stage III, Grade B)
  - Probing depths: ____ sites >= 5 mm; deepest ____ mm
  - Bleeding on probing: ____ %
  - Radiographic bone loss: ____________

CLINICAL CONTEXT (evidence-based, associational)
  Periodontitis is associated with elevated systemic inflammatory
  markers (hs-CRP, IL-6). Periodontal therapy has been shown to improve
  these surrogate markers. I am NOT asserting that this patient's
  periodontal status causes any systemic disease; I am flagging a
  modifiable inflammatory exposure for your consideration.

REQUESTS / SUGGESTIONS
  [ ] Consider baseline hs-CRP (+/- IL-6) if not recently obtained.
  [ ] We are initiating/continuing active periodontal therapy.
  [ ] Suggest re-checking hs-CRP at 3-6 months to track response.
  [ ] Please share relevant cardiometabolic history for coordinated care.

I am happy to coordinate. Thank you for co-managing this patient.

Sincerely,
Dr. ____________, DDS
Contact: ____________
```

The letter models the discipline: it reports objective findings, states the association explicitly, disclaims causation explicitly, and requests concrete, defensible actions.

## Three case vignettes

### Case 1 — Cardiology

A 58-year-old man with hypertension and a family history of early MI has an LDL at goal on a statin, but a persistently elevated **hs-CRP of 4.1 mg/L** with no obvious source. On intake you ask — as this course recommends — about dental care. He hasn't seen a dentist in six years and reports gums that "bleed every time." You refer him for a periodontal evaluation, which returns **Stage III, Grade B periodontitis** with 40% BOP.

*Reasoning:* You have not found the cause of his residual inflammatory risk, and you must not tell him you have. What you *have* found is a plausible, modifiable contributor to an otherwise unexplained hs-CRP. The defensible action is co-management: periodontal therapy plus an hs-CRP recheck at 3–6 months. If the marker falls into the expected 20–30% range, you have quantified a real effect without overclaiming a prevented event.
*Tier check:* "Elevated hs-CRP associated with periodontitis" — Tier 1. "Treating his gums will prevent his heart attack" — Tier 3; do not say it.

### Case 2 — Geriatric / cognitive

An 81-year-old woman with early cognitive decline is brought in by her daughter, who has read online that "gum disease causes Alzheimer's" and wants to know if aggressive dental treatment will halt her mother's decline. The exam and history reveal significant untreated periodontitis and difficulty with oral hygiene.

*Reasoning:* This is a Tier-3 minefield, and the family is standing in it. That *P. gingivalis* gingipains have been detected in Alzheimer's brain tissue is real and provocative — but the gingipain-inhibitor trial failed, and there is no prospective evidence that treating periodontitis alters cognitive trajectory. The right move is honest de-escalation: you cannot promise a cognitive benefit, and should not imply one. You *can* recommend periodontal treatment on its own merits — comfort, function, nutrition, and reduction of a documented inflammatory exposure — which is reason enough.
*Tier check:* "Gingipains detected in AD brain tissue" — Tier 2 observation. "Treating gums prevents/slows dementia" — Tier 3 hypothesis; explicitly correct the family's causal framing.

### Case 3 — Diabetic

A 49-year-old woman with type 2 diabetes has an HbA1c stuck at 8.4% despite good adherence. She also has poorly controlled Stage III periodontitis. Her endocrinologist has optimized her regimen; the number won't budge.

*Reasoning:* The diabetes–periodontitis relationship is bidirectional and among the better-supported oral-systemic links: hyperglycemia worsens periodontal disease, and periodontal inflammation can impair glycemic control. Treating her periodontitis is defensible as an *adjunct* to metabolic management — not a substitute for it. Co-manage with her dental team, treat the periodontitis, and track both hs-CRP and glycemic response.
*Tier check:* "Periodontitis and glycemic control are bidirectionally associated; periodontal therapy is a reasonable adjunct" — supportable. "Fixing her gums will control her diabetes" — overclaim; frame as adjunct only.

## A PCP-intake workflow

You can operationalize all of this in three lines added to your intake:

1. **Ask, at intake:** "When did you last see a dentist, and do your gums bleed?" Two questions, ten seconds, and they flag the patients worth pursuing.
2. **Trigger a marker + referral** when oral history plus stage suggest meaningful exposure — particularly Stage III–IV periodontitis, or a patient with unexplained hs-CRP > 3 mg/L who reports bleeding gums or years without dental care. Order hs-CRP (± IL-6) if not recent; refer for periodontal evaluation.
3. **Close the loop at 3–6 months.** Confirm the periodontitis was actually treated, and re-measure hs-CRP. Document the change. This is the "show that intervention moves the signal" step — the only honest bridge toward any future disease-modification claim.

## Evidence-tier audit of the key claims

Before you take any of this to a patient, run each claim through the tier filter. This audit is itself a course objective.

| Claim you might make | Tier | Verdict |
|----------------------|------|---------|
| "Periodontitis is associated with elevated hs-CRP and IL-6." | 1 — Established | Say it. |
| "Periodontal therapy improves inflammatory and endothelial surrogate markers (hs-CRP often down ~20–30% at 3–6 mo)." | 1 — Established | Say it. |
| "Periodontitis is associated with ASCVD across large cohorts." | 1 — Established (association) | Say it — as association, not causation. |
| "*P. gingivalis* OMVs carry virulence cargo and can activate endothelium / stress mitochondria." | 2 — Supported | Discuss with 'preclinical evidence suggests…' |
| "Gingipains have been detected in Alzheimer's brain tissue." | 2 — Supported (observation) | State as observation, not mechanism-proven-in-humans. |
| "Treating gum disease prevents heart attacks / prevents or slows dementia." | 3 — Hypothesis | Do NOT say it. |
| "One conserved oral mechanism drives vascular, tumor, and brain disease." | 3 — Hypothesis | Research framing only. |

## The takeaway

Putting the mouth back in the H&P does not require believing more than the evidence supports. It requires two intake questions, an occasional inexpensive serum marker, a referral, and a follow-up measurement — all resting on Tier 1 associations and Tier 1 treatment effects. The periodontal exam becomes what it always could have been: a quantifiable, modifiable index of systemic inflammatory exposure that a colleague is already collecting. Order the marker, treat the source, re-measure, and let the number — not a causal story you can't yet defend — do the talking.
