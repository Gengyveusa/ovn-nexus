## Why this unit matters

You already have the science. You can stage periodontitis, you can name the routes out of the mouth, and — after the communication unit — you can say what the evidence supports without claiming a syllable more. But knowing the right thing to say is only half the job at the chair. The other half is whether the person in front of you actually changes anything after they leave.

This is where most oral-systemic conversations quietly fail. A hygienist who has done everything right — accurate staging, honest tier-matched framing, a clean home-care plan — still watches the same patient return in six months with the same bleeding, the same plaque, the same story. The information was correct. The information did not move behavior. That gap is not a knowledge problem. It is a conversation-design problem, and it has an evidence-based solution.

That solution is **motivational interviewing (MI)** — a collaborative, goal-oriented style of communication developed by William Miller and Stephen Rollnick and refined across four decades of clinical trials in addiction, diabetes, cardiology, and, increasingly, periodontal care. MI is not a trick and it is not salesmanship. It is a disciplined method for helping a person resolve their own ambivalence and talk themselves into change. And it marries almost perfectly to the discipline this course has been drilling: MI works *because* it refuses to lecture, argue, or frighten — which happen to be the same three failures that destroy oral-systemic credibility.

> Motivational interviewing is an established behavioral method. The oral-systemic content it delivers still obeys the tier rules. MI changes *how* you say things; it never licenses you to say more than the evidence supports.

Hold that line throughout this unit. MI is your delivery system. The tiers are your speed limit.

## The spirit before the skills

New MI learners reach for the techniques first — the open questions, the reflections — and wonder why they feel mechanical. That is because MI is not fundamentally a set of techniques. It is an underlying **spirit**, and the techniques only work when they express it. Miller and Rollnick describe that spirit with four elements.

- **Partnership.** You work *with* the patient, not *on* them. You are two experts in the room — you on periodontal disease, they on their own life, values, and constraints. The conversation is a collaboration, not a download.
- **Acceptance.** You honor the person's absolute worth, autonomy, and right to choose — including their right to choose not to change today. Acceptance is not approval of the behavior; it is respect for the human.
- **Compassion.** You actively promote the patient's welfare and prioritize their needs. This is what separates MI from manipulation: the goal is genuinely *their* wellbeing, not your production numbers.
- **Evocation.** You draw the motivation *out* of the patient rather than installing it. People are far more persuaded by reasons they discover themselves than by reasons you supply.

That last element is the hinge. The old model — the one most of us were trained in — assumes the patient has a deficit of information that the clinician must fill. MI assumes the opposite: the patient usually already holds the reasons to change, tangled up with the reasons not to. Your job is to evoke and strengthen the former, not to pour more facts onto a person who is already ambivalent.

> The righting reflex — the urge to fix, correct, and set the patient straight — feels like good care and reliably backfires. Every time you argue *for* change, you cast the patient in the role of arguing *against* it.

This is the single most important reframe in the unit. When a patient says "I just don't have time to floss," the trained reflex is to explain why they should make time. MI does the opposite: it gets curious, reflects, and lets the patient supply the argument for change. The moment *you* voice the reasons to change, you have handed the patient the reasons not to.

## OARS: the four core skills

The spirit is expressed through four microskills, remembered by the acronym **OARS**. These are the whole toolkit; everything else is timing.

| Skill | What it is | Oral-systemic example |
|-------|-----------|------------------------|
| **O — Open questions** | Questions that can't be answered "yes/no"; they invite the patient to explore | "What have you noticed about your gums lately?" instead of "Are you flossing?" |
| **A — Affirmations** | Genuine recognition of a strength, effort, or intention — never flattery | "You kept up the interdental brushing through a stressful move — that took real commitment." |
| **R — Reflections** | Restating or rephrasing what you heard, so the patient hears themselves | Patient: "I know it's bleeding, I just get busy." → "So you've noticed the bleeding, and life has made it hard to stay on top of it." |
| **S — Summaries** | Pulling the threads together to show you're listening and to tee up next steps | "Let me make sure I've got this: the bleeding worries you, mornings are your best window, and you'd rather not add another appointment. Did I catch that right?" |

Two of these deserve special attention because clinicians consistently underuse them.

**Reflections** are the engine of MI, not open questions. A good reflection does something a question cannot: it hands the patient's own words back to them slightly amplified, so they can hear their own motivation out loud. Skilled MI practitioners offer roughly two to three reflections for every question. When in doubt, reflect rather than ask.

**Affirmations** must be specific and true, or they read as manipulation. "Good job!" is empty. "You noticed the bleeding yourself and came in — that's you paying attention to your health" names a real behavior and connects it to a value the patient can own.

> Reflections are where MI actually happens. A question asks the patient to produce something new; a reflection lets the patient hear what they already said and decide whether they meant it.

## Change talk: what you are listening for

Everything in MI orbits one target: **change talk** — the patient's own statements in favor of change. Research across hundreds of trials shows that the more change talk a patient voices in a session, the more likely they are to actually change. Your job is to recognize it, evoke more of it, and reinforce it when it appears.

Change talk comes in recognizable forms, easily remembered as **DARN-CAT**:

- **D**esire — "I *want* my gums to stop bleeding."
- **A**bility — "I *could* probably floss at night."
- **R**easons — "If I don't fix this, it'll cost me more later."
- **N**eed — "I *have to* get this under control."
- **C**ommitment — "I'm *going to* start tonight." (the strongest signal)
- **A**ctivation — "I'm *ready* to try."
- **T**aking steps — "I already bought the interdental brushes."

The opposite is **sustain talk** — arguments for the status quo ("I've flossed before, it never made a difference"). Sustain talk is not resistance to be crushed; it is one side of normal ambivalence. The MI move is to *roll with it* — reflect it, explore it, and gently steer back toward change talk — rather than argue against it. When you find yourself in a debate, you have lost the thread: the patient is now defending the very behavior you want them to abandon.

A practical way to evoke change talk without fear-mongering is the **readiness ruler**. Ask: "On a scale of 0 to 10, how important is getting your gum inflammation under control right now?" Whatever number they give — say a 4 — follow with the two questions that do all the work:

1. "Why a 4 and not a 0?" (This forces the patient to voice their own reasons *for* change — pure change talk.)
2. "What would it take to move you from a 4 to a 6?" (This surfaces their own next step, on their own terms.)

Never ask "why a 4 and not an 8?" — that invites the patient to defend why they're *not* motivated, which is sustain talk. The ruler's power is entirely in the direction you point it.

## Meeting the patient where they are: stages of change

MI is usually paired with the **transtheoretical (stages of change) model**, which reminds us that people don't move from unaware to committed in one leap. Matching your approach to the patient's stage prevents the most common error — pushing an action plan on someone who hasn't yet decided they have a problem.

| Stage | Where the patient is | Your MI move |
|-------|----------------------|--------------|
| **Precontemplation** | Not yet seeing a reason to change | Raise awareness gently; ask permission to share information; evoke, don't push |
| **Contemplation** | Ambivalent — sees pros *and* cons | Explore ambivalence; use the readiness ruler; evoke change talk |
| **Preparation** | Intending to act soon | Strengthen commitment; help build a concrete, patient-owned plan |
| **Action** | Actively changing behavior | Affirm effort; problem-solve barriers; reinforce every win |
| **Maintenance** | Sustaining the new behavior | Affirm consistency; plan for relapse without shame |

The clinical payoff is simple: you stop wasting breath. Handing a detailed flossing regimen to a precontemplative patient is like giving directions to someone who hasn't decided to take the trip. Meet them at awareness first.

## Delivering oral-systemic risk without fear-mongering

Here is where MI and the tier discipline fuse. MI tells you to evoke rather than frighten; the tiers tell you exactly how far your claims may reach. Together they produce a way of stating systemic risk that motivates *and* stays honest.

The core reframe is to present the mouth as a **modifiable inflammatory exposure we can reduce** — a Tier-1 framing — rather than a proven cause of a catastrophic event. "Your gums are an inflammatory exposure we can measure and lower" is true, motivating, and within the evidence. "Your gums are giving you a heart attack" is false, frightening, and a Tier-3 claim dressed up as certainty. Fear may spike attention for a moment, but it does not build the sustained, autonomous motivation that MI — and durable behavior change — depends on. It also detonates your credibility the instant the patient fact-checks you.

| Fear-based / overclaim (never) | MI + tier-matched (instead) |
|--------------------------------|------------------------------|
| "Your gum disease is causing your heart problems." | "Research links gum inflammation to higher inflammation throughout the body — and treating it can lower those markers. What are your thoughts on that?" |
| "If you don't fix this, you could get dementia." | "Keeping your gums healthy is one thing that's genuinely in your control for your overall health. Is that something you'd want to work on?" |
| "You have to floss or you'll lose your teeth and end up in the hospital." | "On a scale of 0 to 10, how important is getting this bleeding under control to you right now?" |
| "This is a breakthrough — treating your gums prevents strokes." | "Treating this reduces a measurable inflammatory exposure. That alone is a solid reason to take it seriously." |
| "Trust me, the science is settled — bad gums cause disease." | "The link to whole-body inflammation is well established; the rest is still being studied. Either way, lowering the inflammation is worth doing." |

Notice that the right column never surrenders motivation to stay accurate. It motivates *through* accuracy — an association the patient can verify, an effect on a marker you can actually show them, and a decision handed back to their autonomy. The red-flag words from the communication unit — *causes*, *cures*, *prevents*, *breakthrough*, *the science is settled* — are as corrosive in an MI conversation as in a co-management letter. MI does not relax the speed limit; it just makes the honest version more persuasive than the frightening one ever was.

## A worked before-and-after

The same patient, the same finding, two conversations. The first is competent, informed, and completely counterproductive. The second is MI in tier-matched language.

```
── BEFORE (righting reflex + fear + overclaim) ──
Patient:   My gums bleed a little, but it's always been like that.
Clinician: That bleeding is a real problem. Gum disease is linked to heart
           disease and even Alzheimer's — you really don't want to ignore
           this. You need to floss every day, no exceptions.
Patient:   I've tried flossing before. It's just not realistic with my schedule.
Clinician: You have to make time. This is your health we're talking about.
Patient:   ...Sure. I'll try.        (sustain talk; no real commitment)

── AFTER (OARS + readiness ruler + Tier-1 framing) ──
Patient:   My gums bleed a little, but it's always been like that.
Clinician: You've noticed the bleeding for a while now.        (reflection)
Patient:   Yeah. I guess I figured it was normal.
Clinician: What have you heard about what bleeding gums can mean?   (open Q)
Patient:   Honestly? That it's inflammation. My mom had gum problems.
Clinician: That's exactly it — it's inflammation, and research links it to
           inflammation in the rest of the body. The good news is it's
           something we can measure and lower.                 (Tier-1 framing)
           On a 0-to-10 scale, how important is getting it under
           control right now?                                  (readiness ruler)
Patient:   Maybe a 6.
Clinician: A 6 — why that and not a 2?                          (evoke change talk)
Patient:   Because of my mom, I guess. I don't want to head down that road.
Clinician: So your family history makes this matter to you, and you'd rather
           get ahead of it.                                    (reflection/summary)
Patient:   Yeah. What would actually help?
Clinician: You noticing this and asking — that's the hardest part done.
           (affirmation) What feels doable for you to start?   (evoke a plan)
Patient:   I could probably do the little brushes at night.     (change talk: ability)
```

Read the two side by side. In the *before*, the clinician supplies every reason and the patient supplies the resistance — the roles are exactly backwards. In the *after*, the clinician mostly reflects and asks, and by the end the *patient* is voicing the family reason, the importance, and the plan. Not one word overclaims: the finding stays "inflammation we can measure and lower," never "the thing giving you a heart attack." The motivation is real precisely because the patient generated it and the clinician never had to inflate the science to get there.

> A patient who talks themselves into interdental brushing will do it. A patient you frightened into agreeing will nod, leave, and forget. Evoked motivation outlasts imposed motivation — every time.

## The bottom line

The science in this course earns its keep only if it changes what a patient does after the appointment ends. Motivational interviewing is the established, evidence-based way to close that gap — and it happens to demand exactly the humility the tier discipline already requires. Lead with the spirit: partnership, acceptance, compassion, evocation. Work through OARS, favoring reflections over questions. Listen for change talk, roll with sustain talk, and use the readiness ruler to let patients voice their own reasons. Meet each person at their actual stage of change instead of the one you wish they were in. And when you name the systemic stakes, frame the mouth as a modifiable inflammatory exposure you can measure and reduce — Tier-1, true, and motivating — never as a proven cause of catastrophe. MI is how you say it; the tiers are how far you may reach. Keep both straight and you will do the one thing a perfect exam alone never can: help the person actually change.
