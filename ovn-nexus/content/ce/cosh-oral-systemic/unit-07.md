## Why this unit matters

Everything in the foundations track pointed at a colleague you have not yet met: the patient's physician. You learned that periodontitis generates a measurable systemic inflammatory signal, that periodontal staging reads as a graded index of exposure, and that a handful of serum markers make the signal visible. But the hygienist rarely orders those markers, and never manages the cardiovascular history downstream of them. That work lives in a different chart, in a different building, under a different set of codes. Which means the single most valuable thing you can do with the oral-systemic evidence is not something you do alone. It is something you *initiate* — and then hand off, track, and close.

This unit is about that handoff. It is the clinical-competency version of a skill the foundations lessons treated in principle: turning a chairside finding into a coordinated action that reaches the medical team and comes back with a number attached. You are not being asked to practice medicine. You are being asked to be the person in the building who notices the exposure, documents it in language a physician trusts, and refuses to let the loop stay open.

> The hygienist is frequently the only clinician who sees the patient's mouth on a recall interval, quantifies its inflammatory burden, and can trigger the whole co-management sequence. That is not a small role. It is the initiating one.

Two disciplines govern this entire unit and you should hold them from the first sentence. First, **the tier discipline** — every statement you make to a physician stays associational; you never claim the mouth caused anything. Second, **the closed-loop discipline** — a referral that is never confirmed treated and never re-measured is not co-management, it is a rumor. Both matter. A letter that overclaims is a liability; a letter no one acts on is a waste of the physician's attention and yours.

## When to trigger a referral

Co-management is not for every patient. Most of your recall book has gingivitis or Stage I–II periodontitis that you document, treat, and monitor without ever writing to a physician. The referral pathway activates when the oral inflammatory exposure is large enough, active enough, or chronic enough to be worth a physician's time — and, ideally, when a chairside signal corroborates it.

The trigger is a combination of **stage** and **chair-side signals**, not stage alone. A quiet, stable Stage III mouth in a well-controlled patient is a lower priority than a bleeding, actively progressing one in a patient with cardiometabolic risk sitting in the same chart. Read the two together.

| Trigger tier | What you see at the chair | Referral action |
|--------------|--------------------------|-----------------|
| **Monitor only** | Stage I–II; localized BOP; no systemic risk flags | Document periodontal status; reinforce home care; no letter on this basis alone |
| **Consider referral** | Stage II with high BOP **or** any patient reporting known cardiometabolic risk (hypertension, diabetes, family history of early MI) with generalized BOP | Note the association in the record; discuss with the dentist; letter if risk factors cluster |
| **Refer (standard)** | Stage III; PD ≥ 6 mm at multiple sites; BOP ≥ 30%; active periodontal therapy underway | Author consult letter to PCP; suggest baseline hs-CRP; request cardiometabolic history |
| **Refer (priority)** | Stage IV; extensive bone loss; **or** patient reports an unexplained elevated hs-CRP, statin-resistant risk, or a cardiology relationship | Consult letter to PCP **and** cardiologist; explicit dental–medical co-management; baseline + follow-up markers |

Notice what earns a priority referral: not a worse claim, but a stronger *convergence* of signals. When a patient with Stage IV disease also mentions a cardiologist and a residual risk their statin has not resolved, you are looking at the exact patient in whom a documented, modifiable inflammatory source is most worth surfacing — and the one a cardiologist is most equipped to fold into an existing risk conversation.

> Refer on convergence, not on a single number. Stage tells you the size of the source; the chairside signals and the medical history tell you whether anyone downstream is positioned to act on it.

One caution before you reach for the letterhead. The trigger is a prompt to *communicate a finding and request a defensible action* — it is never a prompt to tell the patient the physician will find the cause of their heart disease in their gums. The referral surfaces a modifiable exposure. It does not diagnose the systemic disease, and you must not let the patient hear it that way.

## The consult letter as a hygienist-authored artifact

The foundations track taught the co-management letter as a dentist-authored document. In practice, the hygienist is very often the one who assembles it — you hold the probing chart, the BOP percentage, the recall history, and the chairside observation that triggered the whole thing. So it is worth building the letter again here, as a standalone artifact you can author and own, keyed to the hygiene workflow.

A defensible letter does four things, in order, and drops none of them:

1. **Reports** the objective periodontal findings — stage-grade, probing depths, BOP, bone loss. Data the physician can verify.
2. **States** the association in plain, sourced, associational language — periodontitis is *associated with* elevated inflammatory markers; therapy *improves* them.
3. **Disclaims** causation explicitly — you are flagging a modifiable exposure, not asserting the mouth caused any systemic disease.
4. **Requests** concrete, defensible actions — a baseline marker, a shared history, a follow-up measurement.

Omit the disclaimer and you have overclaimed. Omit the requests and you have merely gossiped about a patient across a professional boundary. All four, every time — that is what makes the letter credible by construction rather than a liability by construction.

> The four parts are not stylistic. They *are* the tier discipline rendered as a document: report earns trust, association states the evidence, disclaimer holds the line, request converts attention into an action a physician can take on faith in nothing.

Here is the letter, authored from the hygiene chair and adapted so a physician can act on it without believing anything the evidence does not yet support:

```
[Dental Practice Letterhead]
Date: ____________

To: Dr. ____________  (Primary Care / Cardiology)
Re: [Patient name], DOB ____________

Dear Dr. ____________,

I am the dental hygienist co-managing [Patient]'s periodontal care. With
the patient's consent, I am sharing objective findings relevant to their
systemic inflammatory status, for your awareness and possible co-management.

PERIODONTAL FINDINGS (objective)
  - Diagnosis / Stage-Grade: ____________ (e.g., Stage III, Grade B)
  - Probing depths: ____ sites >= 5 mm; deepest ____ mm
  - Bleeding on probing: ____ %
  - Radiographic bone loss: ____________
  - Active therapy: [ ] SRP completed ____________  [ ] in progress

CLINICAL CONTEXT (evidence-based, associational)
  Periodontitis is associated with elevated systemic inflammatory markers
  (hs-CRP, IL-6). Periodontal therapy has been shown to improve these
  surrogate markers, with hs-CRP commonly falling ~20-30% at 3-6 months
  post-definitive therapy.

CAUSATION DISCLAIMER (explicit)
  I am NOT asserting that this patient's periodontal status causes any
  cardiovascular or systemic disease. I am flagging a modifiable
  inflammatory exposure for your consideration.

REQUESTS / SUGGESTIONS (defensible, concrete)
  [ ] Consider a baseline hs-CRP (+/- IL-6) if not recently obtained.
  [ ] We are initiating/continuing active periodontal therapy.
  [ ] Suggest re-checking hs-CRP at 3-6 months to track response.
  [ ] Please share relevant cardiometabolic history for coordinated care.

I am glad to coordinate on scheduling and share our re-evaluation findings.
Thank you for co-managing this patient.

Respectfully,
____________, RDH
[Practice] — supervising dentist: Dr. ____________
Contact: ____________
```

Read that letter the way the physician will. It never asks the PCP or cardiologist to believe anything unproven. It hands over data they can verify, frames it in vocabulary they already trust, and requests actions — a serum marker, a shared history, a follow-up — that are each independently defensible regardless of how the oral-systemic causal question ultimately resolves. That is precisely why it works across the professional boundary: it earns co-management by demanding nothing on faith.

Two adaptations matter for the hygienist author. First, the signature line names your supervising dentist — the letter travels under the practice's clinical authority, and your scope stays clean. Second, when the letter goes to a cardiologist rather than a PCP, the "requests" section is where you defer: a cardiologist manages the residual-risk conversation and the marker interpretation; your job is to supply the periodontal data and the follow-up, not to suggest a cardiac work-up.

## Coordinating with PCPs and cardiologists

The letter opens the loop. Coordination is what keeps it from dying in an inbox. A physician receives a great deal of unsolicited paper, and a dental consult is unfamiliar enough that it can be filed and forgotten unless you make it easy to act on. A few practical moves raise the odds that your finding produces a response.

- **Send it to a named clinician, not "to whom it may concern."** Get the PCP's or cardiologist's name from the patient at the chair. A letter addressed to a person gets read; a letter addressed to a role gets scanned.
- **Match the request to the reader.** A PCP is the right recipient for a baseline hs-CRP and a shared cardiometabolic history. A cardiologist is the right recipient when the patient already carries residual risk on optimized therapy — there, your finding is a candidate contributor to an inflammatory number they are already watching.
- **Give the patient a copy and a script.** The most reliable courier is the patient. Hand them the letter and one honest sentence: *"My hygienist found gum inflammation that can raise a marker your doctor tracks; she's asking whether it's worth a blood test."* That is entirely associational, and it primes the physician before the paper arrives.
- **Log the send date.** Coordination without a date is not coordination. The date you send is the clock you will close against at 3–6 months.

The do/don't-say table below is the vocabulary discipline applied to the co-management conversation specifically — with the physician, with the patient, and in the chart. The left column is defensible today; the right column outruns the evidence and, said to a cardiologist, actively erodes your credibility.

| Do say (Established / associational) | Do not say |
|--------------------------------------|------------|
| "Periodontitis is associated with elevated hs-CRP and IL-6." | "Your patient's gum disease is causing their heart disease." |
| "Treating the periodontitis can lower those inflammatory markers." | "Treating the gums will prevent a heart attack / a second event." |
| "I'm flagging a modifiable inflammatory exposure for your consideration." | "I've found the cause of their residual cardiovascular risk." |
| "We'd suggest a baseline hs-CRP and a re-check at 3–6 months." | "You should start / stop a cardiac medication based on this." |
| "This is one contributor you can actually do something about." | "The science is settled that gum treatment protects the heart." |

The asymmetry is on purpose. Every phrase on the left rests on replicated human data and requests an action the physician can defend. Every phrase on the right requires prospective interventional trials that do not yet exist — and one of them, the medication suggestion, steps outside your scope entirely. When you are unsure which column a sentence belongs in, say the associational version and let the marker do the rest of the talking.

> To a cardiologist, an overclaim is not merely inaccurate — it is disqualifying. The clinician who says "I found the cause of their risk" gets filed under noise. The one who says "I found a modifiable exposure worth measuring" gets a colleague.

## Closing the 3–6-month loop

Here is the discipline that separates co-management from a referral that merely felt productive. A referral that is sent and never followed is an open loop, and an open loop proves nothing. The bridge — the only honest one — toward any future disease-modification claim is the demonstration that **an intervention moved a measured signal.** You cannot walk that bridge without re-measuring.

The loop has three steps, and the hygienist is positioned to own all three because you already control the recall interval on which the last one lands.

| Step | What happens | Who owns it | Timing |
|------|--------------|-------------|--------|
| **1. Baseline** | Physician orders hs-CRP (± IL-6); you record the value in the dental chart | PCP orders; hygienist logs | At/near referral |
| **2. Treat** | Definitive periodontal therapy is completed and confirmed — not merely scheduled | Dental team | Weeks 0–8 |
| **3. Re-measure** | Physician re-checks hs-CRP; you compare to baseline and document the change | PCP re-orders; hygienist tracks | **3–6 months** |

Step 2 is the one most often skipped in spirit. "Referred for treatment" is not the same as "treatment completed," and the re-measurement is meaningless if the periodontitis was never actually reduced. Before you close the loop, confirm the therapy happened — SRP done, re-evaluation performed, inflammation demonstrably lower at the chair. Only then does the marker at 3–6 months mean anything.

And when the marker returns, set the expectation you learned in foundations: randomized trials show hs-CRP commonly falls on the order of **~20–30% at 3–6 months** after definitive periodontal therapy. That is the honest number to anticipate — real, measurable, and modest. It is the size of an effect on a surrogate marker, not the size of a prevented event.

> Prove an upstream signal, show that intervention moves it, and only then discuss disease modification. The re-measurement at 3–6 months is the middle step made literal — and it is the furthest the evidence lets you walk today.

What the closed loop lets you say is precise and defensible: you *measured* the patient's inflammation, *treated* a modifiable source, and *watched the number move.* What it still forbids is the sentence the whole discipline exists to prevent: you did not prevent a heart attack, and a dropped hs-CRP is not a prevented event. Both facts are true at once. The professionalism of co-management lives entirely in keeping them straight — in the letter, in the chart, and in the conversation with the cardiologist who now, because you closed the loop, has a real number to fold into a real risk discussion.

## The bottom line

Co-management does not require the hygienist to practice medicine. It requires you to be the clinician who notices the exposure, documents it in language a physician trusts, and refuses to leave the loop open. Trigger the referral on convergence — stage plus chairside signals plus medical risk — not on a single finding. Author a letter that reports objective findings, states the association, disclaims causation, and requests defensible actions, signed under your practice's authority. Coordinate so the letter reaches a named clinician who can act on it, and match the request to whether that clinician is a PCP or a cardiologist. Then close the loop: confirm the treatment happened, re-measure hs-CRP at 3–6 months, document the change, and expect the honest ~20–30%. Do all of that and you have built the one bridge the evidence permits — from a chairside finding to a measured effect on a surrogate marker — without ever claiming the mouth prevented a disease it has only been shown to be associated with.
