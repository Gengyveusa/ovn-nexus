## Why this unit matters

In Foundations you learned *why* the mouth is not a silo, and in the H&P capstone you watched a physician turn a periodontal chart into a graded index of systemic inflammatory exposure. This unit hands that same instrument to the person who actually holds the probe. You are the hygienist. You see the pockets, the bleeding, the tissue, and the medical history — all in one operatory, on the same afternoon. Nobody in the care team is better positioned to build a per-patient oral-systemic risk stratum, and nobody is better positioned to know its limits.

A *risk stratum* is not a diagnosis and it is not a prognosis. It is a triage layer: a defensible way of sorting the patients in your chair into "watch," "measure," and "co-manage" so that the expensive attention — a serum marker, a referral letter, a physician's phone call — lands on the patients who have earned it. Everything that follows is built to stay inside Tier 1 (Established) and Tier 2 (Supported). We will not tell a patient that treating their gums will prevent a heart attack. We will build a stratum, read the signals honestly, and act at the tier the evidence has actually reached.

> The evidence does not yet support claiming periodontal disease as a validated causal driver of ASCVD, cancer, or Alzheimer's disease. But there is plenty of justified clinical action right now — and you are standing where most of it happens.

## The inputs to a risk stratum

A stratum is assembled from five inputs, all of which you already collect or can collect at the chair. Think of them in two groups: the *oral exposure* variables you measure directly, and the *host* variables that decide how much that exposure matters.

**Oral exposure — what the mouth is doing.**

- **Stage and grade.** Contemporary classification stages periodontitis I–IV by severity and complexity, and grades A–C by rate of progression. Stage is your best single proxy for the *size and chronicity* of the exposure; grade tells you how fast it is moving.
- **BOP%.** The percentage of sites that bleed on gentle probing. This is your *activity* signal — the fraction of the boundary that is open and permeable right now. A stable Stage III mouth at 8% BOP is a different exposure than the same stage at 45%.
- **Deepest PD.** The single deepest probing depth, plus the count of sites ≥ 5 mm, estimates the *ulcerated surface area* in contact with the vasculature.

**Host — how much the exposure matters.**

- **Systemic comorbidities.** Diabetes (especially uncontrolled), established cardiovascular disease or elevated cardiometabolic risk, pregnancy, immunosuppression, and smoking all raise the stakes of a given oral exposure. Diabetes deserves special weight: the periodontitis–glycemia relationship is bidirectional and among the best-supported oral-systemic links.
- **Salivary findings.** Chair-side salivary diagnostics — bacterial-load or specific-pathogen panels (e.g., *P. gingivalis*, *T. denticola*, *T. forsythia*) and host-enzyme assays such as active MMP-8 (aMMP-8) — add a microbial and host-response dimension the probe alone cannot see.

> Read the stratum the way you read a periodontal chart: the oral variables tell you *how large and how active* the source is, the host variables tell you *how much it matters*, and no single number decides the case on its own.

None of these inputs is new to you. What is new is combining them into one deliberate judgment instead of leaving them scattered across the chart.

## Reading the salivary biosensor

Salivary diagnostics are a **Tier 2 — Supported** technology in this context. They are biologically grounded and clinically useful for characterizing the microbial and host-response picture, but they are *adjuncts* to the clinical exam, not replacements for it, and they do not diagnose systemic disease. Read them accordingly.

Two output families matter at the chair:

- **Microbial-load / pathogen panels.** These quantify total bacterial burden or flag specific periodontal pathogens. A high red-complex signal in a Stage III–IV mouth corroborates the exposure you already suspect and can help justify a more aggressive hygiene interval or a periodontist referral. A high signal in a clinically quiet mouth is a prompt to look harder, not a diagnosis.
- **Host-response markers (aMMP-8).** Active MMP-8 reflects the collagen-degrading activity of the host response — tissue breakdown happening *now*. It can flag active destruction before it is obvious on the chart, which makes it useful as an early-activity signal alongside BOP.

The discipline is the same one you apply to any screening test: a salivary result changes your *pre-test suspicion into a post-test judgment* — it does not stand alone. Interpret every salivary output **in the context of the clinical exam**. A pathogen panel does not override a healthy periodontium, and a reassuring panel does not overrule 45% BOP and 7 mm pockets. When a salivary signal and the clinical picture disagree, the clinical picture wins and the salivary result becomes a reason to investigate the discrepancy.

## Co-ordered serum markers: hs-CRP and IL-6

When the stratum warrants it, the co-management step is a serum inflammatory marker, ordered by or coordinated with the physician. Both markers below are **Tier 1 — Established** in this context: their elevation in periodontitis and their improvement after therapy are documented across meta-analyses.

hs-CRP is read against the familiar cardiovascular-risk bands. Memorize them; they anchor the whole conversation.

| Marker | Band | Reading | Chair-side meaning |
|--------|------|---------|--------------------|
| **hs-CRP** | **< 1 mg/L** | Low systemic inflammation | Reassuring; oral exposure, if present, is not producing a high systemic signal today |
| **hs-CRP** | **1–3 mg/L** | Average / intermediate | Watch; re-measure in context, especially if oral exposure is active |
| **hs-CRP** | **> 3 mg/L** | High systemic inflammation | In a Stage III–IV mouth with active BOP, a reasonable prompt to co-manage and treat the modifiable oral source |
| **IL-6** | (less standardized) | Upstream driver of hepatic CRP | Corroborating marker where available; not routinely banded like hs-CRP |

Two rules govern how you use this table.

**First — hs-CRP is non-specific, and you must say so.** It rises with *any* inflammatory stimulus: obesity, a recent cold, an autoimmune flare, a sprained ankle. A single hs-CRP of 4.2 mg/L does not implicate the mouth. What the periodontal exam contributes is a *candidate contributor you can actually do something about*. The marker never proves the mouth is the source; the re-measurement tells you whether treating the mouth moved the number.

**Second — the re-measurement discipline.** Randomized trials of periodontal therapy show hs-CRP commonly falling on the order of **~20–30% at 3–6 months** post-definitive therapy. That is your honest, evidence-based expectation — not a promise, a range. So the loop is concrete: establish a baseline, ensure the periodontitis is *actually* treated, and re-measure hs-CRP (± IL-6) at 3–6 months. A single value is a snapshot; the *paired* baseline-and-follow-up is the evidence.

> Evidence shows periodontal therapy *improves inflammatory markers.* It does **not** show periodontal therapy *prevents heart attacks.* Order the marker, treat the source, re-measure — and let the paired numbers, not a causal story you can't defend, do the talking.

## The stratum → action table

Here is the operational core of the unit. Assemble the inputs, place the patient in one of three strata, and take the tier-matched action. The strata are deliberately coarse — three buckets, not a scoring algorithm — because a triage layer that a busy hygienist cannot run in ninety seconds will not get run at all.

| Risk stratum | Typical picture | Salivary / serum posture | Chair-side action (tier-matched) |
|--------------|-----------------|--------------------------|----------------------------------|
| **Low — Watchful** | Stage I–II; BOP < 10%; deepest PD ≤ 4 mm; no high-stakes comorbidity | Salivary not indicated on this basis alone; no serum order | Document oral-systemic status in the chart; reinforce hygiene; note in medical history; routine recall |
| **Moderate — Measure** | Stage II–III **or** Stage I–II *with* a high-stakes comorbidity (diabetes, established CVD, pregnancy); BOP 10–30%; localized deep sites | Consider salivary panel to characterize burden; suggest **baseline hs-CRP** via physician if other cardiometabolic risk present | Intensify periodontal therapy; send a tier-matched consult note to the PCP; set a re-measure plan; shorten recall |
| **High — Co-manage** | Stage III–IV; BOP > 30% **or** high aMMP-8; deepest PD ≥ 6 mm; often a high-stakes comorbidity | Salivary panel to corroborate; **hs-CRP ± IL-6 at baseline and 3–6 mo** via physician | Active periodontal treatment underway; explicit dental–medical co-management; referral to periodontist as needed; close the loop with paired markers |

Notice what the *actions* are and are not. Every action in the right-hand column is Tier 1: measure, treat, refer, re-measure, document, communicate. None of them attributes systemic disease to the mouth. The stratum decides *who gets the expensive attention*; the tier discipline decides *what you are allowed to say while you give it*.

A word on the boundaries. The single most important dividing line is between **Watchful** and **Measure**, and the thing that most often moves a patient across it is not a periodontal number at all — it is a comorbidity. A tidy-looking Stage II mouth in a patient with an HbA1c of 8.4% belongs in **Measure**, not **Watchful**, because the host has changed how much the exposure matters. Let the systemic history pull patients up a stratum; never let a reassuring salivary panel pull them down past what the clinical exam shows.

## A chair-side decision flow

Run this the same way every time. It is the H&P intake workflow from the capstone, re-centered on the operatory where the probe actually goes in.

```
PERIO EXAM  -->  Stage/Grade, BOP%, deepest PD, sites >= 5 mm
      |
      v
HOST OVERLAY  -->  Diabetes? CVD/cardiometabolic risk? Pregnancy? Smoking?
      |
      +-----------------------------+
      |                             |
   low exposure                high exposure
   AND no comorbidity          OR high-stakes comorbidity
      |                             |
      v                             v
  WATCHFUL                     MEASURE / CO-MANAGE
  document + recall                 |
                                    v
                    SALIVARY PANEL (corroborate burden / aMMP-8)
                                    |
                                    v
                    CONSULT NOTE + hs-CRP (+/- IL-6) via PCP
                                    |
                                    v
                    TREAT periodontitis (active therapy)
                                    |
                                    v
                    RE-MEASURE hs-CRP at 3-6 mo  -->  expect ~20-30% drop
                                    |
                                    v
                    DOCUMENT paired result + coordinate care
```

The flow encodes the whole unit: the exam sizes the exposure, the host overlay decides how much it matters, the stratum picks the action, and the re-measurement closes the loop. The two failure modes to guard against are equal and opposite — *over-measuring* the low-exposure patient with no comorbidity (ordering serum markers nobody needs) and *under-measuring* the Stage III diabetic because the salivary panel looked reassuring. The overlay step exists to prevent both.

## Tier check before you speak

Before any of this reaches the patient, run each statement through the tier filter. This is not paperwork; it is the difference between clinical education and hype, and it is a formal competency of this certification.

| Statement at the chair | Tier | Verdict |
|------------------------|------|---------|
| "Your periodontitis is associated with higher body-wide inflammation we can measure." | 1 — Established | Say it. |
| "Treating your gums often lowers that inflammatory marker by about 20–30% in a few months." | 1 — Established | Say it — as an expectation, not a guarantee. |
| "This salivary panel shows a heavy pathogen load, which fits your clinical picture." | 2 — Supported | Say it — as corroboration of the exam, not a systemic diagnosis. |
| "Your gum disease is causing your heart disease." | 3 — Hypothesis (causal) | Do NOT say it. |
| "If we clean up your gums, we'll prevent your dementia / heart attack." | 3 — Hypothesis | Do NOT say it. |

The patient-facing version of the whole unit fits in two sentences you *can* stand behind: "Research links gum disease to higher inflammation throughout your body, and treating it can help bring those markers down. That's one more reason to take your gum health seriously." Everything the stratum does is in service of earning the right to say exactly that — and no more.

## The bottom line

Risk stratification is the hygienist's version of the physician's dosimeter. You take the four things you already measure — stage/grade, BOP%, deepest PD, and the medical history — add a salivary signal when it earns its place, and sort each patient into Watchful, Measure, or Co-manage. The stratum decides who gets an inexpensive serum marker and a consult letter; the hs-CRP bands (< 1 low, 1–3 average, > 3 high) tell you how to read what comes back; and the ~20–30% post-therapy expectation, measured as a *paired* baseline-and-follow-up, tells you whether treating the mouth actually moved the number.

Hold the two disciplines together and you cannot go wrong. The **stratum** keeps the expensive attention aimed at the patients who need it. The **tiers** keep your mouth honest while you give it. hs-CRP is non-specific — never forget it — so the mouth is a *candidate* you can treat and re-measure, never a proven cause you announce. Build the stratum, read the signals in their bands, act at the tier the evidence has reached, and let the paired numbers do the talking.
